Module 1: AI Today - You're Already an AI User

Estimated time: 10-12 minutes



Introduction: You Are Already an AI User

We often talk about Artificial Intelligence as if it is a futuristic wave coming for our jobs. The reality? You have probably used AI three times before your first meeting of the day.

In your personal life:

Did you unlock your phone with FaceID this morning? That is Computer Vision.

Did Netflix suggest something you actually wanted to watch? That is a Recommendation Engine.

Did your email finish a sentence for you? That is Predictive Text.

In your work:

Has your VLE ever flagged a student as "at risk" before you noticed a problem? That is Predictive Analytics.

Has Turnitin compared a submission against millions of papers? That is Pattern Matching.

Has your inbox sorted messages into "Focused" and "Other"? That is Classification AI.

For years, we have used Predictive AI to help us classify data (for example, "Is this email spam? Yes or No").

What has changed recently - and why we are here today - is the rise of Generative AI. These tools do not just categorise data; they create it. They can write emails, draft lesson plans, summarise messy meeting notes, and generate first drafts of reports in seconds.

But are they reliable? Let us find out.



Before We Begin: The Question on Everyone's Mind

"Will AI take my job?"

Let us address this directly, because it is the elephant in the room.

The AI tools we are learning today are not autonomous agents that can replace human judgement, empathy, or institutional knowledge. They cannot comfort an anxious student. They cannot navigate the politics of a departmental meeting. They cannot tell whether a policy makes sense for your specific context.

What they can do is handle the repetitive, time-consuming parts of your work - drafting, summarising, reformatting, explaining - so you have more time for the work that actually requires a human being.

This course is not about replacing you. It is about reclaiming your time.

Now, with that settled, let us see how much you can trust these tools.



Activity: The "Turing Test" Challenge

[LXD NOTE: Create a 'Quiz' or 'Accordion' interaction here. Do not show the answers immediately. Each scenario should be clickable to reveal the answer.]

Below are three short texts. Your job is simple: Decide if each was written by a Human or an AI.



Scenario 1: The Polite Meeting Decline

"Thank you so much for the invitation. While I appreciate the opportunity to discuss the project, I currently have a conflict in my schedule that prevents me from attending. Please let me know if there are minutes I can review later."

Your Guess: AI or Human?

The Answer: AI (ChatGPT)

Why? Generative AI excels at "corporate speak." It is polite, grammatically perfect, and efficient. It is often more polite than busy humans, who might just write "Can't make it - send me the notes?"



Scenario 2: The Academic Abstract

"The paper argues that constructivist alignment is contingent upon the interplay between learner autonomy and rigid assessment structures, suggesting a bifurcation of traditional modalities."

Your Guess: AI or Human?

The Answer: Human (Academic)

Why? Complexity and jargon are not AI indicators. Humans - particularly academics - often write dense, specialised prose. AI models actually tend to prefer simpler sentence structures unless specifically prompted to be "academic." Do not assume that difficult text must be machine-generated.



Scenario 3: The University Policy

"According to the University's 2024 Assessment Regulation Section 4.2, students are granted a 48-hour automated grace period for all coursework if submitted via the digital portal."

Your Guess: AI or Human?

The Answer: AI - and it is a complete fabrication

Why? This regulation does not exist. The sentence looks perfect. The tone is authoritative. "Regulation Section 4.2" sounds official. But the AI invented the entire thing. This is called a hallucination - and it is the single most important concept in this course.



The Key Lesson: Fluency Does Not Equal Fact

If you got Scenario 3 wrong, you are not alone. Most people do. This brings us to the most important principle of AI literacy:

Just because an AI sounds confident does not mean it is correct.

Generative AI is a prediction engine, not a "truth" engine. It predicts the next likely word in a sentence based on patterns it learned from billions of web pages. Sometimes, to complete a pattern convincingly, it will invent facts, citations, policies, or statistics.



The Hallucination Hall of Fame

This is not a theoretical risk. It has already caused real problems:

Legal disaster (2023): Two New York lawyers submitted a court filing that cited six cases - all invented by ChatGPT. The cases sounded real, with proper citations, but none existed. Both lawyers were sanctioned by the court.

Academic citations: Researchers have found AI regularly invents journal articles, complete with plausible author names, publication dates, and DOI numbers - none of which exist.

Statistics and data: AI will confidently cite percentages, survey results, and figures that it has generated on the spot to make an answer sound authoritative.

The lesson: Never trust an AI-generated fact, citation, or policy reference without verification.

Module 2: What is AI? (Demystification)

Estimated time: 12-15 minutes



Introduction: It's Not Magic, It's Maths

Now that we know AI can fool us, we need to understand how it works. Not because you need to become a computer scientist, but because understanding the mechanism helps you predict when things will go wrong.

If you read the news, you might think AI is a sentient brain that "thinks," "reasons," and "understands" the world. It is not. The AI tools we use today - ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, Gemini - are sophisticated prediction engines. They do not know the answer; they calculate the most likely answer.

This distinction matters. A colleague who knows the answer will tell you if they are uncertain. A prediction engine will confidently give you whatever sounds most plausible - even if it is completely wrong.

To understand this properly, we need to distinguish between the "Old AI" we have used for years and the "New AI" everyone is talking about.



Part 1: Two Types of AI

[LXD NOTE: Use a 'Compare' interaction or a two-column layout here. Consider an animated toggle that lets users flip between the two types.]

Predictive AI (The "Librarian")

You have used this for years, probably without realising it.

What it does: Looks at existing data and sorts it into categories.

How it works: It learns patterns from historical data, then applies those patterns to new data to make a prediction.

Examples in Higher Education:

Tool

What it predicts

Email spam filter

"Is this email spam? Yes/No"

VLE early warning systems

"Is this student at risk of dropping out? Yes/No"

Library recommendation

"Based on what you borrowed, you might like these books"

Timetabling software

"This room configuration minimises clashes"

The output: A label, a score, a recommendation, or a sorted list.

Accuracy: Usually high, because there is often a single right answer based on clear patterns.



Generative AI (The "Creator")

This is the new technology that has everyone talking.

What it does: Creates new content that resembles the content it was trained on.

How it works: It learns the patterns of language (or images, or code) from billions of examples, then generates new content word-by-word based on what is statistically likely to come next.

Examples in Higher Education:

Tool

What it creates

ChatGPT / Copilot / Claude

Text: emails, summaries, explanations, lesson plans

DALL-E / Midjourney

Images: illustrations, diagrams, visual concepts

GitHub Copilot

Code: programming solutions, scripts

NotebookLM

Audio: podcast-style summaries of documents

The output: New text, images, code, audio, or video that did not exist before.

Accuracy: Variable - and this is the critical point. Because it is creating rather than retrieving, it can produce content that sounds perfect but is factually hollow.



The Key Difference

Predictive AI answers questions like: "Which box does this belong in?"

Generative AI answers questions like: "What would a good answer look like?"

That second question is why generative AI is both powerful and dangerous. It is optimising for plausibility, not truth.



Part 2: How Generative AI Actually Works

The "Super Parrot" Analogy

[LXD NOTE: Consider an illustration of a colourful parrot perched on a towering stack of books, papers, and computer screens.]

Imagine a parrot with a perfect memory that has read:

Every book in every university library

Every Wikipedia article in every language

Every public webpage, forum post, and blog

Every open-source code repository

Millions of academic papers, news articles, and social media posts

This parrot does not understand what it has read. It does not know what a "university" actually is, what "ethics" means, or whether something is true or false. But it has developed an extraordinarily sophisticated sense of which words tend to follow other words in different contexts.

When you ask the parrot a question, it does not search a database for the answer. Instead, it predicts - one word at a time - what a good response would look like based on all the text it has seen.



Watching the Parrot Think

If you say to the parrot:

"The cat sat on the..."

The parrot instantly calculates probabilities based on billions of sentences:

Next word

Probability

mat

68%

floor

15%

sofa

8%

windowsill

4%

refrigerator

0.001%

It picks "mat" (or samples from the top options) and moves on. Then it predicts the next word. And the next. Thousands of times per second, until it has built a complete response.

This is why we call it "Generative." It is generating a statistically plausible sequence of words, not looking up facts in a database.

Sentence: "To be or not to..."

Your Prediction: [User types here]

Sentence: "According to the University of Exeter's 2024 policy on generative AI..."

Your Prediction: [User types here]

Why: The AI probably has some information about university AI policies, but it may not have the specific, current policy for a particular institution. If it does not have the data, it will not say "I don't know." It will generate what a policy typically sounds like - complete with invented clause numbers and confident phrasing.

This is the hallucination risk in action.



Part 3: Why Hallucinations Happen

Remember the fake regulation from Module 1?

"According to Regulation Section 4.2, students are granted a 48-hour automated grace period..."

Why did the AI invent this? Because it was not trying to tell the truth. It was trying to complete a pattern.

In its training data:

"Regulation" is often followed by "Section" and a number

"Grace period" is often followed by a time like "48 hours" or "72 hours"

Policy statements typically include specific references

The AI assembled these fragments into a sentence that sounds authoritative but has no factual basis.



The Hallucination Trigger Points

AI is most likely to hallucinate when your request involves:

Trigger

Example

Why it fails

Specific facts

"What is the phone number for Student Services?"

It may not have this data, but phone numbers follow predictable patterns

Recent events

"Summarise yesterday's Senate meeting"

Its training data has a cutoff date; it cannot know recent events

Niche or local information

"What are the opening hours of the Streatham campus library?"

Specific institutional details may be absent or outdated

Citations and references

"Give me three academic sources on cognitive load theory"

It knows what citations look like but may invent plausible-sounding ones

Numbers and statistics

"What percentage of students use AI for assignments?"

It will generate a number that sounds reasonable, not one that is verified



The Golden Rule

The more specific, recent, or local your question, the higher the hallucination risk.

General knowledge questions ("Explain photosynthesis") are relatively safe.

Specific institutional questions ("What does our policy say about...") are high-risk unless you provide the source material.



Part 4: What This Means for Your Work

Understanding how AI works changes how you should use it.

AI is Strong At:

Pattern-based tasks: Drafting emails, reformatting text, explaining concepts in simpler terms

Creative ideation: Generating options, brainstorming titles, suggesting approaches

Summarisation: Condensing long documents (when you provide the source)

Translation and tone adjustment: Making formal text casual, or vice versa

AI is Weak At:

Factual accuracy: Anything requiring verified, specific, or current information

Maths and calculation: Despite appearances, it predicts digits rather than calculating them

Institutional knowledge: Your specific policies, procedures, and context

Judgement calls: Decisions requiring empathy, ethics, or contextual understanding



So, if the Parrot is just predicting words and might make things up, is it safe to use at work?

Yes - but only if you know how to check that you are using a protected version, understand the different modes available, and develop good habits for when to start fresh.

Part 1: The Safety Check (Before You Type Anything)

Why This Matters

There are two versions of most AI tools:

Your institution has provided a protected version specifically to avoid this risk. But you must verify you are using it.

Part 2: Understanding Response Modes

Many AI tools now offer different "thinking" modes. Understanding these helps you choose the right approach for each task.

Standard Mode (Fast Response)

What it is: The default mode. The AI responds quickly using its standard prediction process.

Speed: Near-instant (starts generating within 1-2 seconds)

Best for:

Drafting emails and routine correspondence

Summarising text you have provided

Explaining concepts in simpler terms

Brainstorming ideas and options

Reformatting or restructuring content

Characteristics: Good for volume, may be more superficial on complex problems.



"Think Deeper" / Reasoning Mode (Slower, More Thorough)

[LXD NOTE: The exact name varies by tool - "Think Deeper" in Copilot, "Extended Thinking" in Claude, etc. Emphasise the principle rather than the specific button name.]

What it is: The AI takes longer to "reason" through the problem before responding. It may show its working or thinking process.

Speed: Slower (you may see a "Thinking..." animation for 10-30 seconds)

Best for:

Complex analysis or multi-step problems

Comparing options with trade-offs

Planning or structuring a large piece of work

Tasks requiring logical reasoning

When your first attempt got a superficial answer

Characteristics: More thorough, but uses more computing resources (may have daily limits).

Activity: The "Jargon Buster"

Higher education is full of terminology that confuses new staff, students, and even experienced colleagues from other departments. Let us see if AI can help translate.

Step 2: Ensure you are in Standard mode (the default).

As the response appears, pay attention to:

Analogies: Did it compare scaffolding to something concrete - training wheels, building supports, stepping stones? Good AI explanations often use everyday comparisons.

Length and tone: Is it appropriately simple for the audience you specified? If you had said "explain to a fellow academic," it would respond very differently.

A format (bullet points)

Part 4: The Fresh Start Habit

Why Conversations Get Confused

When you chat with an AI, it remembers everything in that conversation. This context-awareness is usually helpful - you can say "make it shorter" without re-explaining what "it" refers to.

But it can also cause problems:

If you asked about student feedback earlier, then switch to asking about budget planning, the AI might weirdly blend the two topics

Errors or misunderstandings from earlier in the conversation can persist and compound

The AI may make assumptions based on context that no longer applies



The Rule: New Task = New Chat

[LXD NOTE: Insert a screenshot showing the "New Chat" or "+" button location. Note that this interface element may move but the function remains the same.]

When you are finished with one task and starting something different:

Look for "New Chat", "New Conversation", or a "+" button (typically in the top-left or sidebar)

Click it to clear the AI's "memory" of your previous conversation

Start fresh with your new task

This is the equivalent of giving a new brief to an assistant rather than assuming they remember your conversation from three weeks ago.

Develop the habit: When in doubt, start fresh. It costs you nothing but a click.



When to Keep the Same Chat

There are times when maintaining conversation context is valuable:

You are iterating on the same document ("Now make paragraph 2 more formal")

You are having a back-and-forth to refine an idea

You need to reference something you provided earlier ("Using the data I shared above...")

If you are genuinely continuing the same task, stay in the same chat. If you are switching topics, start new.

Module 4: Responsible Use & The "Red Pen"

Estimated reading time: 20 minutes

Introduction: Don't Feed the Parrot Secrets

In Module 2, we learned that Generative AI is like a "Super-Read Parrot" that predicts words based on patterns. Here is the catch: Parrots repeat what they hear.

If you type confidential information into a public AI tool (like the free version of ChatGPT or generic online chatbots), you are effectively sending that data to an external computer. That data might be used to train the next version of the model.

This brings us to the most critical part of AI literacy: Knowing where the "Red Lines" are.



Part 1: The Privacy Red Line

The Golden Rule

If you would not email it to a stranger, do not paste it into AI.

Even with enterprise protection, AI interactions are logged, may be reviewed by IT administrators, and could potentially be subject to legal discovery. Treat the AI like a very efficient but indiscreet colleague.



What Is Personally Identifiable Information (PII)?

PII is any data that could identify a specific individual, either directly or in combination with other information.

Direct identifiers (never input these):

Category

Examples

Names

Student names, staff names, external contacts

ID numbers

Student IDs, staff numbers, National Insurance numbers

Contact details

Email addresses, phone numbers, home addresses

Financial data

Bank details, salary information, bursary amounts

Authentication

Passwords, security answers, access codes

Indirect identifiers (use caution):

These might not identify someone alone, but combined with other information could do so:

Category

Examples

Could narrow to one person

Under UK GDPR, some data requires extra protection. Never input this into AI tools, even protected ones, without explicit guidance from your Data Protection Officer:

Why the extra caution? These categories have elevated legal protection because mishandling them causes serious harm. An AI tool - even a protected one - is not designed or approved for processing this data.



The Anonymisation Option

Sometimes you need AI help with a task that involves real situations. The solution: anonymise before you paste.

Instead of...

Use...

"Draft feedback for John Smith (ID: 12345678)"

"Draft feedback for Student A"

"Summarise the extenuating circumstances for Sarah in Level 3 Biology"

"Summarise the following extenuating circumstances case: [paste details with names removed]"

"Write a reference for Dr James Wilson in Chemistry"

"Write a reference for a Senior Lecturer in a science department with the following achievements: [list achievements]"

The rule: Remove names, IDs, and identifying details before the information enters the AI. You can add them back to the output afterwards.



Part 2: The Bias Red Line

The Mirror Effect

Remember: AI learned to write by reading the internet. The internet contains humanity's knowledge - and humanity's biases.

AI models absorb and reflect the stereotypes present in their training data. This is not a flaw that can be "fixed" - it is inherent to how the technology works.



Bias in Practice: HE Examples

Gender bias:

Ask AI to write a profile of a "professor" and it will often default to male pronouns and stereotypically male interests. Ask for a "nurse" or "teaching assistant" and watch the pronouns shift.

Your responsibility: Review AI-generated content for gendered assumptions, especially in job descriptions, case studies, and example scenarios.

Cultural bias:

AI training data over-represents Western, English-language sources. This affects:

Area

How bias appears

Names in examples

Default to Anglo-American names (John, Sarah) rather than diverse names

Cultural references

Examples assume Western contexts (Christmas breaks, not Eid or Diwali)

Food and lifestyle

"Healthy breakfast" defaults to oatmeal and eggs, not congee, dosa, or fuul

Academic citations

May over-represent Western scholarship, under-represent Global South researchers

Your responsibility: Actively diversify AI-generated examples. Do not accept the defaults as neutral.

Disability bias:

AI may reproduce ableist assumptions or language. Medical-model framing ("suffering from," "confined to a wheelchair") may appear instead of social-model language ("disabled by barriers," "wheelchair user").

Your responsibility: Check AI-generated content about disability for appropriate, respectful language aligned with your institution's inclusive practice guidelines.



The "Reasonable Person" Test

Before using AI-generated content, ask yourself:

"If a student, colleague, or external stakeholder from any background read this, would they feel respected and included?"

If there is any doubt, edit before publishing.



Part 3: The Accountability Red Line

You Own the Output

This is non-negotiable: if you submit, send, or publish AI-generated content, you are responsible for it.

You cannot say:

"The AI wrote it, not me"

"I did not notice the error"

"The tool made a biased assumption"

If your name is on it, you own it. If the AI hallucinates a fake policy, you cited a fake policy. If the AI writes something offensive, you published something offensive.



What This Means Practically

Scenario

Your responsibility

AI drafts an email with an incorrect deadline

You sent an email with an incorrect deadline

AI generates a reading list with fabricated citations

You recommended readings that do not exist

AI writes student feedback with a gendered assumption

You gave biased feedback to a student

AI summarises a policy incorrectly

You provided incorrect policy guidance

The AI is a tool. You are the professional.

Let us apply these principles to a realistic scenario.



Scenario: Alex's Shortcut

Alex is a Department Administrator. It is Friday afternoon, and they are overwhelmed. A sensitive Student Support Committee meeting just finished, and Alex needs to write up the outcomes and notify the student involved.

Alex decides to save time by pasting their rough notes into the AI chatbot to get a clean summary and draft email.



Alex's Prompt:

"Please summarise these notes from the support meeting and draft an email to the student:

Meeting: Student Support Committee, 15 Nov 2024 Present: Dr Harrison (Chair), Alex (Admin), Student Wellbeing Officer

Case: John Smith (ID: 2345678) - Extenuating circumstances application Reason: Severe anxiety and depression following family bereavement Evidence: GP letter confirming diagnosis Request: Extension for all Semester 1 assessments

Committee decision: Application REJECTED - insufficient evidence of impact on specific assessments. Student advised to resubmit with more detail.

Action: Alex to notify student of outcome."



Your Task: Find the Red Flags

Before revealing the answers, identify what Alex did wrong. There are four distinct problems with this prompt.

[LXD NOTE: Give users time to identify issues before revealing. Consider a "Check My Answers" button.]



The Red Flags (Revealed)

🚩 Red Flag 1: Student Name and ID

The problem

Sharing PII (name, student ID) with AI system

The breach

Privacy / Data Protection

Why it matters

Even in a protected system, this creates an unnecessary record linking a named individual to sensitive circumstances

The fix

Remove name and ID before pasting. Use "Student A" or "The Applicant"



🚩 Red Flag 2: Health Information

The problem

Disclosing mental health diagnosis ("severe anxiety and depression")

The breach

Special Category Data under GDPR

Why it matters

Mental health information has elevated legal protection. AI systems are not approved for processing this data

The fix

If summary is essential, generalise: "health-related circumstances" without diagnostic detail



🚩 Red Flag 3: Bereavement Details

The problem

Including personal context ("family bereavement")

The breach

Privacy - unnecessary personal detail

Why it matters

The AI does not need this context to summarise a decision. Including it creates unnecessary record of sensitive personal circumstances

The fix

Omit. The AI only needs to know there was an EC application and the outcome



🚩 Red Flag 4: Asking AI to Draft a Rejection

The problem

Delegating sensitive communication to AI

The breach

Accountability / Human-Centricity

Why it matters

A student receiving a rejection while experiencing mental health difficulties needs human empathy, not machine-generated correspondence. This is exactly the kind of communication that requires human authorship

The fix

AI could draft a template for EC outcome letters. But this specific letter, to this specific student in these specific circumstances, should be written by a human who can judge the appropriate tone



The Corrected Approach

If Alex genuinely needs help, here is a responsible version:

"Draft a professional email template for notifying a student that their extenuating circumstances application was unsuccessful, with an invitation to resubmit with additional information. The tone should be supportive and constructive."

Then Alex:

Receives a generic template

Personalises it with appropriate care and empathy

Reviews for tone given this student's specific situation

Sends it as their own professional communication

Time saved: Still significant (template drafting). Risk: Eliminated.

AI Augmentation, Not Automation

The safest way to use AI is to keep yourself in the decision-making loop.

AI Automation (risky)

AI Augmentation (safer)

AI drafts, human reviews and sends

AI decides

AI presents options, human decides

AI replaces judgement

AI informs judgement

1. Transparency

If you use AI to create content for students or colleagues, acknowledge it.

Examples:

"Drafted with AI assistance, reviewed and edited by [Name]"

"Initial structure generated using Copilot, content verified against [source]"

This is not about shame - it is about honesty. Many institutions are developing norms around AI acknowledgement. Check your local guidance.

2. Accountability

You are responsible for the output. Always.

This means:

Verify facts before sharing

Check for bias before publishing

Ensure tone is appropriate for the recipient

Confirm policy references are accurate

3. Data Minimisation

Only give the AI what it needs. Nothing more.

Module 5: The Decision Matrix (To Use or Not to Use?)



Module 5: The Decision Matrix

Estimated time: 15-18 minutes

Prerequisites: Modules 1-4 (Understanding AI, hallucination risk, safe access, and ethical red lines)



Introduction: Two Different Questions

You now understand the Red Lines - what you must never input into AI. But responsible AI use requires answering two separate questions:

Can I use AI for this? (Is it safe and permitted?)

Should I use AI for this? (Is it effective and appropriate?)

Something can be permitted but still be a bad idea. For example:

Asking AI to calculate exam grade boundaries is not a privacy breach, but it is a bad idea because AI is unreliable at maths

Asking AI to draft a bereavement condolence message is not illegal, but it may be inappropriate because such messages need genuine human empathy

This module gives you a practical tool - the Decision Matrix - to answer both questions quickly, every time.



The Decision Matrix: Three Gates

Think of this as a traffic light system. Before using AI for any task, run it through three "gates" in order. You must pass all three to proceed safely.

[LXD NOTE: Consider presenting this as an interactive flowchart or a three-step accordion. The visual metaphor of traffic lights is deliberate - universally understood and memorable.]

Making It Automatic

The goal is for this decision process to become instinctive. Within a few weeks of practice, you should not need to consciously run through each gate - it will become automatic, like checking your mirrors before changing lanes.

Building the Habit

Week 1-2: Deliberately pause before each AI use. Mentally run through the three gates.

Week 3-4: Notice when you are about to skip the check. Slow down and verify.

Week 5+: The assessment becomes automatic. You will feel uncomfortable when something does not pass the gates.

Module 6: Prompting Basics (The R-C-T Framework)

Estimated reading time: 20 minutes

Introduction: Garbage In, Garbage Out

Remember the parrot analogy from Module 2? If you ask a parrot to "Say something," it might squawk random noise. If you ask it to "Recite the first line of Hamlet," it knows exactly which words to predict.

If you do the same with AI, that is usually not the AI's fault. It is a "prompting" fault.

The Golden Rule: The quality of the AI's output is directly tied to the specific details in your input.



The Secret Formula: R-C-T

You don't need to be a "Prompt Engineer" to get good results. You just need a simple structure to guide the AI. We call this the R-C-T Framework.

[Use a graphic or 'Hotspot' image showing these three puzzle pieces locking together.]

1. Role (Who is the AI?)

Tell the AI who to pretend to be. This sets the "perspective" of the text.

Examples: "Act as a strict editor," "Act as a sympathetic student support officer," "Act as an Excel expert."

2. Context (What is the background?)

Give the AI the information it doesn't know. Who is the audience? What is the goal? What are the constraints?

Examples: "This is for a frantic email to staff," "The audience is first-year international students," "I need to reduce this text by 50%."

3. Task (What exactly do you want?)

Be specific about the format. Do you want a list? A table? A polite email? A code snippet?

Examples: "Draft a 3-paragraph email," "Create a bullet-point summary," "Create a table with columns for Name and Date."



Worked Example: The Makeover

Let’s look at the difference R-C-T makes.

❌ The "Lazy" Prompt:

"Write an email about the library renovation."

The Result: The AI will write a generic, boring email. It might invent dates. It might be too formal or too casual. It doesn't know if the library is closing for a day or a year.

✅ The R-C-T Prompt:

[Role]: Act as the Head Librarian. [Context]: We are closing the main floor for painting next week (Monday-Wednesday). The quiet study rooms remain open. The audience is stressed students preparing for exams. [Task]: Draft a short, reassuring email notifying them of the closure. Emphasize that study spaces are still available. Keep it under 200 words.

The Result: The AI writes a specific, empathetic, and accurate draft that requires very little editing.
